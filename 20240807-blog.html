Motivation

7 August 2024

In my first ever blog entry (which you almost certainly won't have read), I mentioned how I've tried teaching myself to code a few times, but alway dropped out or burnt out after a few months, even though I enjoyed myself throughout. I've spent a few weeks reflecting on this, and I think I've learnt a few useful things about myself.

Here are some of the mistakes I think I've made.

1. Not prioritising having fun

OK, so this might sound like a bit of a twee one to start with. But it kept cropping up when I was self-reflecting: my primary motivation has always been to end up doing this as a career.

I do tend to think of coding as a creative hobby, no different really to other creative pursuits like learning to play an instrument or painting. I mean, we're making stuff out of nothing, aren't we? How many people start learning to play the guitar with the sole aim of joining a band and becoming a professional musician? I'm not talking about someone dreaming, 'Wow, wouldn't it be great if I was in a touring rock band' - I mean getting famous being the only reason they practise.

I produce my own electronic music under the name rquit. I enjoy it. I'm not too bad at it. And funnily enough, I've also burnt myself out on this numerous times too. Why? Well, I followed the same trajectory as my previous coding burn-outs:

  1. Start doing it for fun, and have loads of fun
  2. The thought crosses your mind, 'Imagine doing this for a living!'
  3. Immediately start trying to hit professional standards do it for a living
  4. Stop having fun
  5. Get disheartened and quit

Maybe there are some people who can have these lofty ambitions and priorities and also still enjoy themselves. But I am not one of those people. So I need a different motivation. If I can keep enjoying myself, keep learning, and maybe take part in some community projects once I'm experienced enough, that's still time well spent.

2. Setting unrealistic goals

This one follows on quite nicely from that. The second I decide I want to start doing something to a professional standard (which, I should state, is not a bad thing in and of itself), I draw up a silly, unrealistic schedule which immediately adds unnecessary self-imposted pressure: 'If I work really hard and study for 3 hours a day, I could probably get myself job-ready in six months.'

This can cause burnout in a number of ways: it sucks the fun out of coding, because suddenly you're treating this fun thing as more of a second job; you're setting yourself up for disappointment when you're still working your boring office job after six months.

(As a brief aside, it's really worth really highlighting for beginners that you can't just learn one language and then do everything you want with it. When I first tried learning C#, I held the misconception that this one language was all I needed. I remember finishing my C# fundamentals course (big shout out to Bob Tabor, you are the man) and then thinking how I was ready to build my first android app. So I booted up Visual Studio and was immediately hit by the realisation, 'Oh god I now have to learn a couple of other frameworks and markup languages before I can even make a start?!')

Other than just pure mental burnout, unrealistic goals around timescales are also dangerous because you can easily fall into the trap of rushing through learning just so you can tick off having 'completed' that language or that project (the benefits of being an 'active learner' is covered in the next section). So you've got all this internal pressure on yourself, and then as you're studying away, you realise you've not relly understood the things you've been trying to learn anyway because you were rushing.

3. Being a passive learner

At least in my case, this mistake is a direct result of the first two mistakes.

When you're determined to learn everything there is to learn in an unrealistically short space of time, you quickly end up being a passive learner. This is where you read an article or follow a tutorial about a particular concept, finish it and then think, 'OK cool, I know how that works now. On to the next topic.' You rush because you're desperate to get onto the next topic.

But it's when you become an active learner that you start to really understand topics. Reading the article, following along with the examples provided, then trying to build your own examples to play around with what you've just learnt - that's how you build understanding. When you look at the example code and think, 'I wonder what would happen if I changed this thing? I bet I could break it,' or, 'That's given me a really good idea for a project I could build - I'll make a note of that and have a go once I've finished up this lesson.'

Passive learners get stuck in tutorial hell because they need someone to tell them exactly what to do, so they never go off and just try things out on their own.

It's important to note that tutorials are not bad - when you're learning a new topic, you obviously need someone to explain things and tell you exactly what to do, because you don't know it yourself yet. But you can still engage with that tutorial rather than just mindlessly skim it and then move on.

Conclusion

So I'm doing things differently this time around. OK, so I might still end up burning out anyway, but I've already built this cool static blog site. And I'm following a roadmap that fully lays bare how little I know, how much I have to learn, and how long it's going to take. But at least I'm going in with my eyes wide open this time.

I've also consciously spent longer working through my HTML/CSS/JavaScript courses, because I'm actively doing my own stuff around it and playing around with things as I learn them (even though I'm really excited to reacquaint myself with C# when I get to some backend stuff!).

And as a final reflection: I have to make peace with the fact that I will most likely never get to make this my job. But I know that the process of learning is fun, and that can be an end in itself. It's not 'failure' if i'm not an employed developer within an arbitrary period of time - it's an almost certainty.

And that's OK.
